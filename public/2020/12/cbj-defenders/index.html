Blue on Blue: An analytical look at the Columbus defense

December 12, 2020 in

The Blue Jackets entered the 2019-2020 season with several major questions, the most pressing was whether two unproven goalies could replace the consistent, Vezina-level play of Sergei Bobrovsky. Joonas Korpisalo and Elvis Merzlikins proved themselves by providing solid goal tending for most of the season and actually helped improve the team’s Goals Against Average from 2.82 to 2.61. None of this occurred in a vacuum however, and a large part of that improvement can be attributed to the defensive corps in front of them and their focus on limiting dangerous attempts. And while it’s easy to see the contributions of a player like Seth Jones on tape, quantifying this for every player is much less straightforward. In an attempt to better connect this eye test with data, I followed Haider Hussain’s process for [grouping NBA players by role] (https://towardsdatascience.com/using-k-means-clustering-algorithm-to-redefine-nba-positions-and-explore-roster-construction-8cd0f9a96dbb) to identify the different ways defenders in the NHL play and see where the Blue Jacket players fit.

As the base for this identification, I used 2019-2020 player stats from Hockey Reference, creating a dataset with 17 stats (converted to per 60 minutes where applicable) for the 256 players with at least 120 minutes of ice time. After standardizing each series with z-scores, Principal Component Analysis was applied to get three factors from the data. Using these weights, shown in the table above, sacrifices a large portion of the variation found in the original data but has the benefit of reducing everything to three dimensions, broadly representing offense, defense, and finishing ability. The new variables created from these weights are then used to create six groups with k-means clustering. The following tables present notable players from each of these groups.

Norris Candidates

The Norris Candidates group contains the most recognizable names and for good reason. These defenders rate out as the best in all three categories, capturing their ability to dictate play and put the puck in the back of the net.

Two-Way Players

This group is similar to the Norris Candidates in terms of defensive impact but lacks some of the offense and finishing skill. That said, these players still provide value at both ends of the ice.

Stay-at-Home Defenders

These players may not be as recognizable as the previous two categories but they still have a positive impact on the defensive end. They can’t be relied upon on at the other end of the ice however, as they rate out negatively on offense and finishing.

Forwards Playing Defense

This group is the mirror image of the Stay-at-Home Defenders, as they rank second best in terms of offense and finishing but have a negative impact in their own zone. Interestingly, a lot of players typically perceived as Norris candidates ended up listed here, as their offensive impact masks some of their deficiencies on the other side of the puck.

Bottom Pair Players

These players are rated as the worst defenders of all six groups and provide almost as much negative value in the offensive zone. There are a few surprising names on this list, which may have just as much to do with the difficulty of estimating defensive impact as their ability.

Healthy Scratches

The smallest of the six, this group is made up of players without a lot of games played. They have a lot of variance but generally struggle on both sides of the puck.
